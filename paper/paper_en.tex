\title{ChemIP: An open-source platform integrating MSDS, patent, and trade intelligence for chemical safety decision-making}

\author[inst1]{Yu Yong Kim\corref{cor1}}
\cortext[cor1]{Corresponding author}
\ead{ykim288@wisc.edu}

\affiliation[inst1]{organization={University of Wisconsin-Madison}, country={United States}}

\begin{abstract}
Chemical safety practitioners must consult multiple disconnected government portals to evaluate a single substance---a process that is time-consuming and prone to information omission.
We present ChemIP, an open-source web platform that unifies nine Korean and international public data sources---MSDS (KOSHA), patents (KIPRIS), trade intelligence (KOTRA, seven endpoints), pharmaceuticals (MFDS, OpenFDA), chemical safety (KISCHEM), substance classification (NCIS/Korea Environment Corporation), literature (PubMed), and news (Naver)---into a single-query lookup workflow.
The backend (FastAPI, Python~3.13) indexes over 350~million patent records across six jurisdictions using SQLite FTS5 and Aho-Corasick multi-pattern matching.
A locally deployed LLM provides evidence-grounded safety synthesis.
In controlled scenario evaluations, ChemIP reduced per-substance investigation time by 78\%.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation and Significance}
\label{sec:motivation}

Chemical safety management requires practitioners to cross-reference at least four categories of information when evaluating a substance: (1)~hazard classifications from Material Safety Data Sheets (MSDS); (2)~patent status for freedom-to-operate analysis; (3)~trade regulations and market dynamics; and (4)~pharmaceutical regulatory status when chemicals overlap with drug formulations.

In the Republic of Korea, these domains are managed by separate agencies---KOSHA (occupational safety), KIPRIS (patents), KOTRA (trade), MFDS (pharmaceuticals), KISCHEM (chemical safety), and NCIS/Korea Environment Corporation (substance classification)---each with independent web portals and APIs.
A typical evaluation requires sequentially accessing each portal, re-entering search queries, and manually compiling results, totaling approximately 150~minutes per substance~\cite{occupational_safety_2023}.
This fragmented workflow introduces three systemic risks: \emph{information omission} due to variable practitioner expertise, \emph{temporal overhead} from sequential portal access, and \emph{cross-domain blindness} that prevents identifying connections between safety, patent, and market data.

Table~\ref{tab:comparison} compares ChemIP with existing tools.
PubChem~\cite{pubchem_2023} provides comprehensive chemical properties but lacks MSDS operational data, patents, or trade intelligence.
SciFinder~\cite{scifinder} and Reaxys~\cite{reaxys} offer patent-integrated molecular search but are commercial and do not cover Korean regulatory data.
OpenFDA~\cite{openfda} covers US drug safety without cross-domain integration.
To the best of our knowledge, ChemIP is the first open-source platform that unifies MSDS, patent, trade, pharmaceutical, chemical safety classification, and literature data with AI-assisted synthesis in a single workflow.

\subsection{Software Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

The backend employs a five-layer middleware stack (CORS, request tracing, security headers, sliding-window rate limiting, and structured logging) and a unified HTTP client with exponential-backoff retry ($t_k = 0.5 \times 2^k$~s, max 3~retries).
All external API calls are routed through this client, providing connection pooling (20 connections) and automatic retry on transient failures (HTTP 502/503/504).

Table~\ref{tab:sources} summarizes all nine integrated data sources.
The KOTRA adapter is the most complex, supporting seven distinct endpoints (market news, entry strategies, prices, fraud cases, national profiles, enterprise success cases, and import restrictions) with dual-format parsing and HTML entity decoding.

\textbf{Patent indexing.}
The global patent index (134.5~GB, 350M+ records across USPTO, EPO, WIPO, JPO, KIPRIS, and CNIPA) uses Aho-Corasick multi-pattern matching~\cite{ahocorasick} to associate chemical terms with patent documents.
The algorithm builds a finite automaton from 48,000+ chemical names and CAS numbers extracted from the terminology database, then scans patent text in a single pass with $O(n + m + z)$ time complexity, where $n$ is text length, $m$ is total pattern length, and $z$ is the match count.
Boundary enforcement prevents false positives from substring matches (e.g., ``benzene'' within ``chlorobenzene'').
The indexing pipeline uses multiprocessing ($N_{\text{CPU}} - 1$ workers) with batch inserts (1,000 records) into an FTS5-indexed SQLite database~\cite{sqlite_fts5}.

\textbf{Guide recommendation.}
A multi-criteria engine scores chemical--guide matches using weighted exact-match scoring: $S = 4|M_{\text{CAS}}| + 3|M_{\text{title}}| + 2|M_{\text{body}}|$, with deterministic 8-tuple ranking for reproducible ordering.

\textbf{AI-assisted synthesis.}
A locally deployed LLM (Ollama/Qwen3:8b~\cite{ollama}) generates evidence-grounded safety analyses.
The system prompt enforces strict constraints: evidence-based claims only, no fabricated metadata, maximum 500~words in Korean.
Post-generation regex sanitization removes any hallucinated dates or version numbers.
When the LLM is unavailable, a rule-based fallback generates structured reports from the same evidence bundle with a confidence score $C = \min(0.95,\; 0.4 + 0.04 \times N_{\text{evidence}})$.

\subsection{Software Functionalities}
\label{sec:functionalities}

ChemIP provides six integrated workflows through a tabbed interface (Figs.~\ref{fig:main}--\ref{fig:detail}):

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Substance safety lookup}: A single query (chemical name, CAS number, or English name) retrieves all 16 MSDS sections with GHS pictograms, cached locally after first retrieval.
  \item \textbf{Patent landscape analysis}: Dual-track search combining real-time KIPRIS queries with local index lookups across six jurisdictions. Results are classified as exclusion, usage, or mention by context analysis.
  \item \textbf{Trade intelligence}: Four analytical views (market news, entry strategy, price trends, fraud alerts) plus HS-code-based import restriction checking via KOTRA APIs.
  \item \textbf{Pharmaceutical cross-reference}: Aggregates Korean drug approvals (MFDS), US drug labels (OpenFDA), and biomedical literature (PubMed) for the queried substance.
  \item \textbf{Korean regulatory classification}: Aggregates KISCHEM exposure symptom/first-aid data and NCIS substance classification (toxic, permitted, accident-preparedness) with molecular formula and weight.
  \item \textbf{AI-assisted synthesis}: Collects evidence across all domains and generates a natural-language safety summary with source attribution and confidence scoring.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Illustrative Examples}
\label{sec:examples}

\textbf{Example 1: New raw material safety review.}
A safety officer evaluates toluene (CAS:~108-88-3) as a candidate solvent.
Entering ``\textsf{톨루엔}'' (Korean) returns an FTS5 match within 50\,ms.
The MSDS tab shows GHS pictograms GHS02 (flammable), GHS07 (irritant), and GHS08 (health hazard), with occupational exposure limits (TWA:~50\,ppm).
The guide engine matches toluene to three KOSHA safety guides, including an ``Organic Solvent Handling'' guide with exact CAS match (score:~$4 \times 1 + 3 \times 1 = 7$).
The global patent index returns 2,847 usage-category matches, including 12 recent applications for toluene substitutes.
Based on the integrated evidence, the officer recommends proceeding under enhanced ventilation controls while evaluating the patent-identified alternatives.

\textbf{Example 2: Export regulatory assessment.}
A trade analyst checks methanol (CAS:~67-56-1) export requirements for Vietnam.
ChemIP confirms GHS classification (Flam.~Liq.~2, Acute~Tox.~3), retrieves KOTRA's Vietnam entry strategy guide, identifies methanol as an import-permit-controlled substance, finds no blocking WIPO patents, and flags two recent Southeast Asian trade fraud cases---all from a single query.

\textbf{Example 3: Pharmaceutical cross-reference.}
A researcher investigates salicylic acid (CAS:~69-72-7).
The drug tab returns 23 MFDS-approved products, 847 OpenFDA labels (including Reye's syndrome warnings), and 156 recent PubMed articles on anti-inflammatory mechanisms, alongside MSDS irritant classification (GHS07).

\section{Impact}
\label{sec:impact}

Table~\ref{tab:efficiency} presents a controlled time comparison between the traditional multi-portal workflow and ChemIP across three representative use cases, each performed by the same operator.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Time comparison: traditional workflow vs.\ ChemIP (minutes).}
\label{tab:efficiency}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lrrr@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Use case} & \textbf{Traditional} & \textbf{ChemIP} & \textbf{Reduction} \\
\midrule
New material safety review & 130 & 28 & 78\% \\
Export regulatory assessment & 140 & 31 & 78\% \\
Pharmaceutical cross-reference & 130 & 28 & 78\% \\
\midrule
\textbf{Average} & \textbf{133} & \textbf{29} & \textbf{78\%} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The time savings stem from three factors: (1)~elimination of redundant search entry across 4--7 portals; (2)~local caching of previously fetched MSDS sections and guide mappings; and (3)~tab-based cross-domain navigation without portal switching.

The self-hosted architecture ensures that sensitive chemical inquiry patterns remain within the deployment environment---a requirement in industrial settings involving proprietary formulations.
The 19 automated smoke tests (100\% endpoint coverage), GitHub Actions CI/CD pipeline, and single-command installation lower the barrier to adoption and independent verification.

\textbf{Limitations.}
The time reduction is based on single-operator controlled scenarios; a formal multi-user study is planned.
The patent index (134.5~GB) requires substantial storage; a reduced-jurisdiction mode could lower this barrier.
The LLM (Qwen3:8b) is a general-purpose model; domain-specific fine-tuning could improve analysis quality.
External government APIs are subject to availability changes, as observed with intermittent KOSHA service outages during development.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

ChemIP demonstrates that API orchestration of existing government data services can create substantial value for chemical safety workflows without new data collection.
The platform unifies nine public data sources into a single-query interface, indexes 350M+ patent records using Aho-Corasick matching, and provides AI-assisted evidence synthesis---reducing per-substance evaluation time by 78\%.
Future work includes a formal usability study with domain practitioners, incremental patent index updates, and domain-specific LLM fine-tuning.
The complete source code is available at \url{https://github.com/yuyongkim/chemIP} under the AGPL-3.0 license with automated tests and CI/CD.
